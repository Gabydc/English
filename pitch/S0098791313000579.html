    Volume 39, Issue 2, June 2013, Pages 97–104

    E-books in Academic Libraries: Challenges for Discovery and Access

    • William H. Walters,
    • Bowman Library, Menlo College, 1000 El Camino Real, Atherton, CA 94027, USA
    Available online 11 May 2013

    Abstract

    This paper examines the recent literature on the presentation of e-books in college and university libraries, focusing on three mechanisms for discovery and access: e-book vendors' interfaces, library catalogs (OPACs), and resource discovery tools (RDTs). If libraries rely on vendors' interfaces, patrons must search on multiple platforms, many of which have limited and idiosyncratic search mechanisms. The most common strategy for discovery and access—including e-books in the library catalog—brings its own set of challenges, including limited availability of records, lack of standardization, difficulties managing the addition and removal of titles, and the generally low quality of vendor-supplied records. Likewise, libraries that use resource discovery tools face another set of difficulties: incomplete coverage, reliance on metadata from external sources, problems with subject headings and authority control, difficulties with guest-user access, and continuing dependence on vendors' platforms for access to full text.

    Keywords

    • Catalog;
    • E-book;
    • MARC;
    • OPAC;
    • Resource discovery;
    • Vendor-supplied records

    1. Introduction

    Almost all academic libraries maintain collections of e-books (Library Journal, 2012 and Wilkie and Harris, 2010). At the same time, many librarians have been tentative in their e-book acquisitions, confining their e-book collections to reference works or to specialized subject areas. Fifty-six percent of university libraries spend less than 6% of their acquisitions budgets on e-books, and only 6% spend 25% or more. The average academic library expenditure on e-books increased by just 3.7% from 2011 to 2012, and the median expenditure actually declined by 5.1% (Library Journal, 2012).

    Both librarians and publishers have acknowledged that inadequate mechanisms for discovery and access are a serious barrier to the large-scale adoption of e-books (Albanese, 2007, Cox, 2004, Library Journal, 2012, Slater, 2010 and Wilkie and Harris, 2010). In particular, many students have difficulty identifying the e-books they need. A recent large-scale survey revealed that nearly half of all postsecondary students have never used a library e-book, and 47% of non-users report that they do not know how to find e-books in the library (ebrary, 2011). Similar results have been reported for a wide range of institutions (Cassidy et al., 2012, de Oliveira, 2012 and Smyth and Carlin, 2012).

    This paper examines the recent literature on e-book discovery and access, with special emphasis on the challenges associated with large-scale e-book cataloging in the academic environment.1 Most of the works cited here have appeared in peer-reviewed journals, and the discussion relies on systematic rather than anecdotal evidence whenever possible. Relevant papers were identified through searches of LISTA (Library, Information Science & Technology Abstracts), LISA (Library and Information Science Abstracts), Library Literature & Information Science, Google Scholar, and WorldCat.

    E-books are defined here as book-length monographic works that are presented chiefly as text and intended for online delivery within a multi-user environment. They include current editions, backlist titles, and archival works. Here we will focus on the discovery and presentation of e-book files (titles or works) rather than e-book readers (devices), mainly because so few of the e-books held by academic libraries are compatible with commercially available e-book readers (Ahlroos and Hahto, 2012, Kemp et al., 2012 and Kiriakova et al., 2010). The difficulty of maintaining e-book readers prohibits their use at many institutions (Anthony, 2012 and Watters, 2012), and most of the academic libraries that do lend e-readers have chosen not to catalog either the devices or the titles that reside on them (Clark, 2009, Rodzvilla, 2009 and Walters, 2013b).

    The paper begins with a discussion of two difficulties that are independent of access mechanism: the existence of multiple levels of aggregation for each e-book (Section 2.1) and the non-standard assignment of e-book ISBNs (Section 2.2). The discussion then focuses on three major mechanisms for e-book discovery and access: the online interfaces developed by e-book vendors (Section 3), the catalogs (OPACs) maintained by librarians (Section 4), and the resource discovery tools that can be used to search a wide range of information resources (Section 5). Each of these mechanisms can meet patrons' needs for discovery (identification of the e-books that are available and potentially relevant) and access (retrieval of the corresponding full-text files). At the same time, each mechanism has distinct advantages and disadvantages.

    2. Difficulties that are Independent of Access Mechanisms

    2.1. Multiple Levels of Aggregation

    Although librarians have expressed a strong preference for title-by-title selection, most e-books are acquired through large collections that represent broad subject areas or particular publishers (Library Journal, 2012 and Vasileiou et al., 2012a). In at least some cases, the titles in these collections are aggregated, acquired, and cataloged as if each book series were itself a single work. In other cases, individual works (books) are further disaggregated into sections or chapters, each of which is cataloged separately (Kirchhoff, 2011). These multiple levels of aggregation mean that libraries or individual scholars may find and acquire a single chapter, a single e-book, or a book series.

    For example, the SpringerBriefs online publishing program has at least five levels of aggregation: series, subseries, sub-subseries, monographic title, and chapter. Libraries may acquire content at any one or more of these levels. As a result, librarians and even vendors' staff sometimes find it difficult to determine the relationships among the various levels of aggregation. Even the simple question “Does the library have access to this text?” becomes difficult to answer conclusively.

    2.2. Nonstandard Assignment of E-book ISBNs

    E-book ISBNs (International Standard Book Numbers) pose challenges that sometimes defeat the purpose of using the ISBN as a unique identifier (Delquié and Polanka, 2011 and Wood et al., 2010). For printed books, each version—hardcover or softcover—is assigned a unique ISBN by the publisher. That process works well when the distinctions between each version are readily and widely understood. It works less well for e-books, however, since the distinctions between the different versions of an e-book are not always clear-cut. Moreover, e-book ISBNs can be assigned both by publishers, who own the content, and by aggregators, who package and market e-books to libraries. (The major e-book aggregators include dawsonera, EBL, ebrary, EBSCO, and MyiLibrary.) In some cases, the publisher assigns a single ISBN to a digital work that is subsequently distributed to several vendors for use on their proprietary platforms. Each vendor may add vendor-specific formatting or features, but each of the several versions retains the single ISBN that was assigned by the publisher. In other cases, the publisher declines to register an ISBN on the assumption that each vendor will assign one. Some vendors then add their own ISBNs, while others are content to release their e-books without them. This may reflect the fact that the registration of ISBNs requires a payment. As Dillon (2011, p. 13) has pointed out, “Many smaller publishers are less than eager to purchase blocks of ISBNs to assign to each and every format of every book.” In addition, some publishers reuse the same ISBNs for multiple e-books, making them both useless and potentially misleading (Morris & Sibert, 2011).

    3. Difficulties Associated with the Use of E-book Vendors' Interfaces

    Most e-book vendors (publishers and aggregators) maintain online databases of the titles they make available to libraries.2 Typically, the vendors' platforms can be tailored to show just those e-books available through a particular library or consortium. The advantage of this discovery/access mechanism is that the responsibility for maintenance lies with the e-book supplier rather than the library staff.

    There are several disadvantages to the use of vendors' interfaces, however. As noted earlier, patrons must search multiple databases—one for each e-book vendor—in the hope that one of them will have an e-book that meets their needs (Su, 2005). This is especially problematic when no one vendor offers a critical mass of e-book titles (Link, 2012). In many cases, patrons must also create and maintain a separate account and password for each e-book vendor. This problem, which first became apparent with the original NetLibrary platform, persists even today (Arch, 2012 and Hodges et al., 2010). As Albanese (2012, p. 39) has asked, “Just how many plug-ins and systems and steps should a casual reader be expected to navigate just to get an e-book?”

    Many e-book interfaces lack advanced search capabilities, subject headings, and authority control, so patrons' searches are limited in their flexibility and precision. Some platforms lack classification schemes and browsing capabilities, making it difficult for patrons to find e-books similar to known items of interest. Moreover, the library must provide the necessary infrastructure even when the vendor maintains the database itself. The updating of browser settings and essential plug-ins can be a significant undertaking, especially for universities that support multiple operating systems and browsers (Gedeon and Meyer, 2005 and Walters, 2013b).
